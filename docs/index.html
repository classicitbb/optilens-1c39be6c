🔍 Russell's Observation Sheet — Note These During the Dry Run

Was Sandra greeted warmly and by name within 30 seconds of arrival?
Did the front desk explain what would happen next — or just hand her a form?
Was Sandra told what the eye test involves before she went in?
Was there a clear, warm handoff from the front desk to the optometrist?
After the eye test, did Sandra know her results in plain language?
Was there a clear handoff from the optometrist to the frame consultant?

Debrief & Feedback

🎤 Speaker Note — The Feedback Framework

Always lead with what went well. Then move to gaps. Never use the word "wrong." Use the phrase: "The gap here is between what we did and what Sandra needed." This is professional, not personal.

Debrief Script
Russell
"Thank you. That was genuinely valuable — and brave. Let's talk through what I observed."
Russell
"First — what went well." [Share 2–3 specific positives from your observation sheet. Be specific. "The greeting was warm and immediate" is better than "the greeting was good."]
Russell
"Now — the gaps. And I want to be clear: these gaps exist because no one has shown us exactly how to close them. That is what this morning is for."
Russell
[Work through your specific observations. For each gap, name the moment, describe what happened, describe what was needed. Then say: "We'll talk about exactly how to handle that in the next section."]
Russell
"The most important moment in that entire scenario — the one that will make or break a sale AND a patient's experience — was the moment Sandra was led to the frame rack. That handoff. That transition. That is the moment everything either comes together or falls apart. And that is where we are going to spend a lot of our time today."
⚠️ Things to Flag if They Came Up
  • "Have a look around" — this is not guidance. A patient who wanders is a patient we've abandoned.
  • Silence between stages — every handoff needs a sentence. "Sarah is going to take great care of you from here."
  • Price mentioned too early or too vaguely — the patient should never be surprised by cost at the counter.
  • Technical language without explanation — "myopic" means nothing to Sandra. "Short-sighted" does.

The Dentist Model

🎤 Speaker Note

This is the philosophical core of the entire session. Take your time here. Use the analogy slowly and let it land before moving on.

Introducing the Dentist Model
Russell
"I want to tell you about the most successful optical experience you can give a patient. It looks exactly like a visit to the dentist."
Russell
"When you go to the dentist, you don't decide your treatment. The dentist examines you, explains what you need, guides you through each step with calm authority — and at the end, you pay. There's no confusion, no wandering, no pressure. You feel completely looked after. You trust them."
Russell
"That is what PricesMart Optical must be for every single patient. A place where they are guided. Where every stage is clear. Where they never feel lost, pressured, or confused. Where at the end — all they need to do is pay."
Russell
"We are kind. We are warm. We are encouraging. But we are also authoritative. We know what the patient needs better than they do — because we are the professionals. And we must act like it."
The Dentist Model in One Sentence

"The patient follows the professional's lead at every stage — with warmth, with clarity, and with confidence. At the end, they pay. That is the experience we build."

What "Kind & Authoritative" Looks Like

  • Warm greeting, patient's name used
  • Clear explanation of each next step
  • Frames pre-selected and presented — not browsed
  • Lens recommendation given, not asked
  • Confident pricing — no apology for cost
  • Encouraging language throughout

What Breaks the Model

  • "Have a look around, let me know"
  • Silence between stages / no handoff
  • Waiting for the patient to ask questions
  • Agreeing with a patient just to avoid conflict
  • Mixed messages between staff members
  • Price as a surprise at the counter

The 7-Stage Store Flow

🎤 Speaker Note

Walk through these stages on the floor if possible. Physically move through the space as you describe each stage. This makes it real and spatial.

1

The Appointment — Phone or Walk-In

Set the tone before the patient arrives. On the phone: warm, efficient, informative. "Your visit will take about 45–60 minutes. Please bring any existing glasses or prescription if you have one." Walk-in: greet immediately, check capacity, make them feel they are expected even if they are not.

2

Arrival & Welcome

The patient should be greeted by name (or introduced to) within 30 seconds of entering. "Welcome to PricesMart Optical — we've been looking forward to seeing you." Seat them, offer water, explain what happens next. Do not just hand them a form in silence.

3

Pre-Test Briefing

Remove mystery. Before the patient enters the testing room, briefly explain: "We'll be checking your vision clarity, eye health, and prescription. It takes about 15–20 minutes and nothing is uncomfortable." Lead them to the room — do not point.

4

The Eye Test

The optometrist leads. Clear, calm instructions. After the test — explain the findings in plain language. Not "you have mild myopia" — say "you're a little short-sighted, which means things in the distance are slightly blurry for you." Then, the critical handoff: "Sandra, I'm going to introduce you to [Name] who is going to help you choose a frame that works perfectly for your prescription."

5

The Frame Opportunity — ⭐ The Most Important Stage

This is where most optical practices lose the patient. Do not say "have a look around." Lead with intention. Before bringing the patient to the rack, pre-select 3–5 frames based on what you know about their face shape, skin tone, and lifestyle. "I've already pulled a few that I think will really suit you — let's try these first." Guide their feedback: "How does that feel? Would you like something bolder, or softer?" You are the expert. Act like it.

6

Lens Consultation

Once the frame is chosen: "Wonderful — now let's talk about the lenses, which is where the real magic happens for your vision." Offer two options, not ten. Tie recommendations to lifestyle: "Do you spend a lot of time on a screen? Then I'd recommend our blue-light filter — you'll notice the difference by the end of the first day." Recommend confidently. Don't ask "do you want X?" — say "I recommend X because…"

7

Payment & Goodbye

By this point, the patient knows the total. There is no surprise. Recap warmly: "So you've chosen [frame] with [lens type and coating]. Your lenses will be ready in [X] working days and we'll give you a call." Then: care instructions, cleaning cloth, case, and a confident close. "You've made a really good decision today — you're going to love the difference."

✅ The Golden Rule of Handoffs

Every time a patient moves from one person to the next, there must be a sentence. "Sandra, this is Lerato — she's going to take excellent care of you from here." The patient must never feel transferred or abandoned. They must feel passed, with care, into the next pair of capable hands.

Lenses & Frames Knowledge

🎤 Speaker Note

This is the product knowledge section. Keep it practical — everything tied to a real patient conversation. For each lens type, ask: "How would you explain this to Sandra in one sentence?"

Lens Types

Lens Type What It Does Plain-Language Explanation for Patients Best For
Single Vision One prescription — distance or near only "These lenses are focused for one distance — either seeing clearly far away or up close." Most patients under 40; first-time wearers
Bifocal Two prescriptions — visible dividing line "These have two sections — the top for distance, the bottom for reading. You'll see a small line across the lens." Patients who need both distances but prefer cost-conscious option
Progressive Full range of vision, no visible line "These give you clear vision at all distances with no line — it's a seamless transition from far to near." Patients 40+ needing reading and distance; anyone wanting natural vision correction

Lens Coatings & Add-Ons

Anti-Reflection (AR)

  • Reduces glare from screens, headlights, lighting
  • Makes lenses nearly invisible — more natural look
  • "This eliminates the glare you'd otherwise see on every lens. It's the most noticeable upgrade."

Blue-Light Filter

  • Filters high-energy light from screens
  • Reduces eye fatigue, improves sleep
  • "If you're on a phone, tablet, or computer for more than 2 hours a day — this one is for you."

Photochromic (Transitions)

  • Darkens in sunlight, clears indoors
  • Acts as both clear glasses and sunglasses
  • "One pair does everything — no need to carry a separate pair of sunglasses."

UV & Scratch-Resistant

  • UV blocks harmful sun exposure to the eye
  • Scratch-resistant extends lens life significantly
  • "These protect both your eyes and your investment."

Frames — Face Shape Guide

Oval Face

  • The most versatile face shape
  • Almost any frame style works
  • Encourage the patient to try different styles freely

Round Face

  • Angular, rectangular, or geometric frames
  • Creates definition and lengthens the face
  • Avoid round or small frames

Square / Strong Jaw

  • Round, oval, or curved frames soften angles
  • Cat-eye styles work well
  • Avoid boxy or angular frames

Heart / Wide Forehead

  • Wider at the bottom, lighter at the top
  • Rimless or semi-rimless frames are flattering
  • Avoid very heavy top-bar frames
⚠️ The Frame Fit Truth Principle

There is only ONE legitimate reason to tell a patient a frame will not work: if it physically cannot be fitted to their prescription or face correctly. Bridge too narrow, temples too short, lens too small for the prescription — these are real reasons. Personal taste is not a reason to say no. Never buckle simply because a patient hesitates. And if you must say a frame won't work — always follow immediately with: "But I have something that gives you the same look and will fit you perfectly."

Patient Communication

🎤 Speaker Note

This section is about language. Real words. What to say, what never to say. Do a quick group exercise: ask the team to come up with the worst thing they could say at the frame rack. Laugh together. Then show them the better version.

The 3-Step Communication Model

Acknowledge what the patient says. Guide them toward what they need. Confirm they are comfortable moving forward. Every interaction, every time.

Say This, Not That

"I don't know."
"That's a great question — let me find out for you right now."
"Have a look around and let me know."
"I've already picked a few I think will suit you beautifully — shall we start here?"
"That doesn't really suit you."
"Let me find you something that suits you even better — I have an idea."
"That's not my department."
"Let me get the right person to help you with that — one moment."
"It depends." (and nothing more)
"It depends on a couple of things — let me ask you a quick question so I can give you the right answer."
"The cheapest option would be…"
"Let me show you what's available at different price points and explain the difference, so you can make the best choice for you."
"Do you want the coating?"
"Based on what you've told me, I'd recommend the anti-reflection coating — here's why it will make a real difference for you."

Encouraging Language — Use These

✅ Encouragement Phrases
  • "That looks really good on you."
  • "You've made a great choice."
  • "You're going to love the difference in your vision."
  • "You've made a really good decision coming in today."
  • "That colour works beautifully with your skin tone."
  • "A lot of our patients choose that option and they're always happy with it."
⚠️ The Truth Principle — Non-Negotiable

Tell the truth. If you don't know something — say so and go find out. If a frame truly does not work for a patient's prescription or face — tell them, with an immediate alternative. But do not lie to a patient, do not agree with them just to avoid friction, and do not let a patient leave with something that is wrong for them simply because you did not want the uncomfortable conversation. Our patients trust us. That trust is earned through honesty, every single time.

Browsers, Rejection & the Conversion Gap

🎤 Speaker Note — Lead With the Real Problem

This is where you name the elephant in the room. PricesMart has traffic. It has members. It has free eye tests bringing people through the door. But those people are leaving without buying. That is a conversion problem — and it starts at the front door, not at the frame rack.

The PricesMart Dilemma

We are fully booked with free eye tests. The footfall is there. The trust is there — these are members. The gap is not in traffic. The gap is in what happens between the moment someone walks in and the moment they walk out. That gap is where revenue lives — and right now, we are leaving it on the table.

Part 1 — The Three Types of Visitors

🔍 The Browser

Came in curious. Not sure they need anything. Looking at frames, displays, prices. No appointment. Could become a buyer with the right invitation.

📋 The Eye Test Patient

Has an appointment. Is already inside the system. Has a real clinical need. Should be your highest-conversion visitor — but often isn't.

🔄 The Returner

Has bought before or been in before. Knows the store. May be comparing, browsing upgrades, or ready to buy again. Treat them like a valued guest, not a stranger.

Part 2 — Engaging the Browser: The N·A·I Method

🎤 Speaker Note

There are two mistakes staff make with browsers: ignoring them completely (hoping they'll ask for help) or pouncing immediately (triggering defensiveness). The answer is neither. It is NAI — Notice, Acknowledge, Invite.

N

Notice — Within 30 Seconds

The moment someone enters or begins browsing, a staff member must notice them. Make eye contact, give a genuine smile, and nod. This signals: you are seen, you are welcome, there is no pressure. You do not need to move yet. Just acknowledge their presence.

A

Acknowledge — A Warm, Pressureless Opener

Within about 60 seconds, approach calmly with an opener that is not "Can I help you?" — that question almost always triggers "No, I'm fine, just looking." Instead, use an observation or a gift of information. See the examples below.

I

Invite — Plant a Seed, Then Step Back

Leave one piece of value — a piece of information, a gentle offer, a question — and then genuinely step back. Do not hover. Give them space to breathe. The invite is not a demand. It is an open door. Most people will walk through it when they feel safe.

Better Browser Openers — Use These Instead of "Can I Help You?"

"Can I help you?" / "Are you okay there?"
"Welcome in — take your time. I'll be just around if you'd like to try anything on."
(Ignoring completely until called)
"Those frames just came in this week — they've been really popular. Let me know if you'd like to see them up close."
"We have a deal on right now if you're interested."
"Are you a PricesMart member? Your eye test is actually completely free — worth doing while you're here."
"What are you looking for today?"
"Are you replacing a pair or is this your first time looking at glasses?" (This opens a conversation, it is not a sales pitch)

Part 3 — Handling "I'm Just Looking" or "I'm Okay, Thanks"

💡 Key Insight

"I'm just looking" is not a rejection. It is self-protection. It means: "I don't want to feel pressured." The correct response removes the pressure without removing yourself from the conversation. The wrong response is to apologise, disappear, or push harder.

The Plant · Space · Return Method

Plant one piece of value and step back warmly. Space — give them genuine room to browse without hovering. Return naturally after 2–3 minutes with something specific and non-pushy. By the third interaction, the wall is usually down.

Script — Handling "I'm Just Looking"
Visitor
"Thanks, I'm okay — just looking."
Staff
"Of course, take all the time you need — no rush at all. I'll just mention, if anything catches your eye and you'd like to try it on, I'm happy to pull it out for you." [Smile. Step back. Do not hover.]
Staff
[2–3 minutes later, approach naturally with something specific — not "can I help now?"] "That style you were looking at is one of our lighter frames — a lot of people are surprised how comfortable they are. Would you like to try it on? It costs nothing to see how it looks."
Staff
[If they're a member or the topic arises:] "Just so you know — as a PricesMart member your eye test is free, no appointment needed for a check, we can often fit you in today. Even if you're not sure about glasses, it's worth knowing where your eyes are at."
Script — If They Are Clearly Not Buying Today
Visitor
"Thanks, I'm really just browsing. I might come back."
Staff
"Absolutely, you're always welcome. Can I ask — are you looking for something specific, or just exploring styles? Sometimes it helps to know what you're working with — even if you're not ready to buy today." [This keeps the conversation open without pressure and gathers useful information.]
Staff
"If you haven't had your eyes tested recently, it's worth doing before you commit to frames anyway — that way you know exactly what lens you'd need. And it's free for members." [Plant the free eye test as the low-commitment next step.]
⚠️ What Never to Do With a Browser
  • Don't offer a discount immediately — it signals desperation and undermines value before they've even seen the product
  • Don't hover — staying within 2 feet of someone who said "just looking" will drive them out of the store
  • Don't give up after one "no" — the first response is almost always reflex. The second and third interactions are where real conversations begin
  • Don't use emotional pressure — no urgency manufactured from thin air ("this deal ends today"). That damages trust.

Part 4 — The Conversion Gap: Free Eye Test → Frame Sale

🎤 Speaker Note — This is the Business-Critical Piece

These patients have already said yes. They came in, they booked, they showed up, they sat in the chair. They trust PricesMart enough to let us test their eyes. And yet they're leaving without glasses. That is not a traffic problem. That is a handoff problem, a language problem, and a bridge problem. Walk the team through this carefully.

①

The Eye Test Must End With a Bridge, Not a Full Stop

Right now the eye test ends and the patient leaves — or wanders. The optometrist must close every single test with a deliberate handoff sentence that creates a natural next step. Not optional. Not "if they want." Every time. "Your prescription is [X]. The good news is there are some really great frames here that will work beautifully with this prescription — let me introduce you to [Name] who can help you find the right fit."

②

The Optometrist Is the First Salesperson — They Just Don't Know It

During the eye test, ask lifestyle questions: "Do you drive at night? Spend a lot of time on screens? Work outdoors?" These answers become the frame consultant's roadmap. The optometrist should briefly tell the patient: "Based on what you've told me, you'd really benefit from an anti-reflective coating — that will make a big difference for night driving." Now the patient arrives at the frame rack already primed for a specific recommendation. The consultant doesn't start from zero.

③

The Prescription in Hand Is a Buying Signal — Use It

A patient who leaves with a prescription and no glasses will take that prescription somewhere cheaper. They will compare. The way to prevent that is to make the conversation happen before they leave. Not aggressively — just logically: "You already have your prescription right here, and we have frames that work with it right there. You could walk out today seeing clearly — it's worth 10 minutes to look."

④

Address the Price Moment Before It Becomes an Objection

Many patients don't buy because they assume they can't afford it — but they never actually asked. The frame consultant must bring price into the conversation early and without embarrassment. "We have options across a few different price ranges — shall I show you what's available so you can see what works for you?" This removes the fear of being surprised and keeps the patient in the room. Never let price be the reason someone left without asking.

⑤

The "Not Today" Patient — Plant for the Return

Some patients genuinely cannot buy today — budget, time, decision-making. That is fine. The goal is to make sure they come back to us, not go elsewhere. Close warmly: "No problem at all. Your prescription is valid for [X months]. When you're ready, come back and we'll pick up right where we left off — I'll have a few frames in mind for you." If possible, take their name. Make it personal. That patient is not lost — they are deferred.

✅ The Conversion Mindset in One Statement

Every person who comes through the door for a free eye test is a warm lead. They have already trusted us with their eyes. The only question is whether we give them a reason to trust us with their glasses too. That reason is service, confidence, and a clear invitation — not pressure, not discounts, not silence.

2:45 — 3:00 · 15 Minutes

Q&A Preparation

🎤 Speaker Note

These are the questions likely to come from the floor. Be ready with confident, honest answers. You can share these answers openly — transparency builds trust with your team.

What cleaner should I be recommending patients use on their lenses?
Always recommend our in-store lens spray with a clean microfibre cloth. Advise patients to avoid anything containing alcohol, ammonia, or acetone — these strip coatings. Never use paper towels, tissues, or clothing — these cause micro-scratches. Rinse with water first if there is dust or grit before wiping.
I've heard our suppliers have changed — is that true? What does it mean for patients?
Acknowledge any changes honestly. "Yes, we've made some adjustments to our supply partners to ensure we can offer consistent quality and availability for our patients." Do not speculate if you are unsure of the details. If a patient presses: "Your lenses are manufactured to the same optical standards — what matters is that your prescription is correct and your lenses are produced accurately, which they are."
How long will the lenses take? / What's the current turnaround time?
Always give a range, never a single day that can become a broken promise. Example: "Standard lenses are typically ready in [X–Y] working days. If your prescription requires specialist lenses, it can be up to [Z] days — and we will always let you know upfront." Call the patient proactively when ready — do not wait for them to follow up.
What if a patient says they can't afford the recommended lens option?
Do not immediately drop to the cheapest option. First: "Let me walk you through the difference — that way you can make the best decision for you." Explain what they gain and what they give up at each price point. If they still choose the lower option, support that choice warmly. Never make a patient feel judged for their budget.
What if a patient insists on a frame that I know isn't right for their face?
Do not simply agree to avoid conflict. Say: "I want to show you one more option before we decide — I think you'll really like what I have in mind." Present an alternative enthusiastically. If they still insist — and the frame physically fits the prescription and their face — respect the choice and support it. The only hard no is a frame that genuinely cannot be fitted correctly.
📋 Open Q&A From the Floor

After covering the above, open the floor for any additional questions. Keep answers concise. If you don't know the answer: "That's a great question — let me get you the right answer rather than guess. I'll follow up by [date]."
